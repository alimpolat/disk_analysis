HARD DISK FAILURE ANALYSIS

by Alexander Bastidas Fry

Thesis

Although there are many observables correlated with hard disk failure, all predictive models of when a given hardrive will fail are not precise.

Motivation

Component failure in large-scale IT installations is becoming an ever larger problem as the number of components in a single cluster approaches millions. Avoiding downtime and the cost of actual downtime make up more than 40% of the total cost of ownership for modern IT systems

Background

90% of all new information produced in the world is being stored on magnetic media, most of it on hard disk drives. Large-scale installation field usage appears to differ widely from nominal datasheet Mean Time to Failure (MTTF) conditions. The field replacement rates of systems were significantly larger than we expected based on datasheet MTTFs. Predictable failures, result from slow processes such as mechanical wear and gradual degradation of storage surfaces. Monitoring can determine when such failures are becoming more likely. Unpredictable failures, happening without warning and ranging from electronic components becoming defective to a sudden mechanical failure. The majority of failures are mechanical of some sort Failure analysis of computer hardware is also of interest in high perfomance computing and distibuted computing where designing highly dependable systems requires a good understanding of failure characteristics. . Many researchers have pointed out the importance of analyzing failure data and the need for a public failure data repository, one such reposity for HPCC is The Computer Failure Data Repository (CFDR), unfortnately it has not seen any contirbutions to its database since 2009. A significant body of work on analyzing hardware failures in server machines exists, but there is a dearth of information on consumer machines, even though they constitute the vast majority of machines sold and used each year.
